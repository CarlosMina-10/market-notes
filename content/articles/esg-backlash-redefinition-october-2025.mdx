The political temperature around ESG investing in the United States reached new highs in 2025. A wave of state-level legislation restricting the consideration of ESG factors in public pension fund investments expanded from its origins in Texas and Florida to a broader coalition of Republican-governed states. Several large asset managers — responding to political pressure and to the threat of losing public fund mandates — walked back or rebranded their ESG commitments. The phrase "ESG" itself became something close to a liability in certain institutional marketing contexts, replaced by softer formulations like "sustainable finance" or "long-term value investing."

Meanwhile, across the Atlantic, European institutional investors were moving in precisely the opposite direction. The European Central Bank accelerated its climate risk stress testing framework. Several major Scandinavian and Dutch pension funds increased their minimum ESG threshold requirements for portfolio inclusion. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, which took effect for large companies in 2024, began producing the kind of standardized data that had previously made rigorous ESG analysis difficult.

## The Political Debate Is Obscuring the Risk Question

The dominant framing of the ESG debate in U.S. financial media has become almost entirely political — conservatives versus progressives, red states versus blue states, "woke capitalism" versus fiduciary duty. This framing is not only reductive; it is actively obstructing a more important conversation about risk.

The fundamental question that ESG analysis was designed to answer is not a values question. It is a risk question: do environmental, social, and governance factors create material financial risk for companies over a medium to long-term investment horizon? And the honest empirical answer, across a growing body of research, is: sometimes yes, sometimes no, and the quality of the data matters enormously.

The problems with ESG as it was practiced — inconsistent ratings across providers, greenwashing by issuers, correlation between ESG scores and sector biases that produced unintended concentration — were real. Critics who identified these problems were right. But the political response of discarding ESG entirely has moved the conversation from "how do we fix the methodology" to "climate risk isn't a financial risk," which is demonstrably incorrect.

## Climate Risk Is Financial Risk — Full Stop

Physical climate risk — flooding of production facilities, drought impacts on agricultural supply chains, sea level rise affecting coastal real estate — is not an environmental philosophy. It is a property rights issue, a supply chain issue, and an insurance pricing issue. The insurance market is already making this adjustment: in 2025, several major insurers reduced or eliminated coverage in high-risk coastal markets in the United States, creating financial risk for asset owners that is direct, measurable, and not contingent on anyone's political views.

Transition risk — the financial impact of moving from a carbon-intensive economy to a low-carbon one — is similarly real whether you support the transition or oppose it. Companies with significant stranded-asset exposure in fossil fuel infrastructure face balance sheet risk that will materialize regardless of political sentiment, because the energy transition is being driven by technology cost curves and energy security imperatives as much as by climate policy.

The European institutional investors doubling down on ESG are not doing so primarily for ideological reasons. They are doing so because their regulatory environment requires disclosure, their beneficiaries have extended time horizons, and their risk models are updating to incorporate the evidence accumulating on physical and transition risk.

## **Market Notes Take**

ESG was always imperfect. The ratings methodologies were inconsistent. The disclosure was unreliable. The marketing was sometimes outright dishonest about what "sustainable" funds actually held. Critics who pointed out these problems were identifying real issues that needed to be fixed.

But gutting ESG entirely in favor of short-term returns ignores something fundamental: climate risk is financial risk. Full stop. The property damage, the supply chain disruption, the stranded assets, the insurance repricing — these are happening in the real economy right now, and they are showing up in quarterly earnings reports, not just in environmental advocacy papers.

The market will learn this the hard way, as it always does. The political moment that is making "ESG" unspeakable in certain institutional contexts will not make the underlying risks disappear. When those risks materialize — and the insurance market is already pricing that they will — the communities on the front lines of that lesson didn't choose to be there. They tend not to. That is also worth putting in the risk model.

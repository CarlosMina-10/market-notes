The transmission mechanism is not subtle. When the Federal Reserve maintains interest rates at elevated levels — as it has through much of 2024 and into 2025 — the interest rate differential between dollar-denominated assets and local-currency alternatives widens. Capital, which optimizes for risk-adjusted returns across borders, responds accordingly. Outflows from emerging market portfolios accelerate. Local currencies weaken against the dollar. Import costs rise. And because most Latin American economies import a significant portion of their consumer goods — including essential foodstuffs, energy, and manufactured inputs — that currency weakness translates directly into higher domestic prices.

This is not a theoretical model. It is a pattern with multiple empirical iterations, most recently visible in the inflation profiles of Colombia, Chile, Peru, and Brazil in 2023 and 2024, where central banks were compelled to maintain domestic rates well above what their inflation outlooks would have required in isolation, specifically to defend currency stability and limit the import inflation channel.

## Monetary Sovereignty Is a Fiction for Small Open Economies

The textbook model of monetary policy assumes that a central bank sets rates based on domestic inflation and output gap conditions, and that the exchange rate adjusts as an equilibrating mechanism. For large, closed economies with deep capital markets, this model is approximately correct. For small, open economies with significant dollar-denominated debt and limited financial account depth, it is a convenient fiction.

A central bank in a small Latin American economy that cuts rates when domestic inflation is at target but the Fed is holding rates elevated will face immediate capital outflow pressure, currency depreciation, and — through the import price channel — renewed inflation. The domestic monetary policy signal is overwhelmed by the external financial conditions signal. In practice, this means that several LatAm central banks have had to keep their own rates higher than their domestic fundamentals warranted, with real economic costs: slower credit growth, compressed business investment, and higher debt service costs for households and firms.

The people bearing those costs — the household that cannot access credit for a small business expansion, the worker whose real wage erodes as import prices rise — did not vote in the FOMC meeting that set the rates creating their problem.

## The Architecture of International Finance Is Decades Out of Date

The post-Bretton Woods international monetary system was designed for a world where capital flows were limited, the U.S. economy was the dominant driver of global demand, and the financial integration of developing economies was minimal. That world ended sometime in the 1990s. The architecture, mostly, has not been updated.

The tools for international monetary policy coordination are inadequate. The IMF has surveillance capacity but no enforcement mechanism for spillover effects. Bilateral swap lines are extended at the Fed's discretion and reflect U.S. geopolitical priorities as much as economic need. The G20's coordination mechanisms are procedurally important but practically limited in their ability to constrain the unilateral monetary policy of the world's reserve currency issuer.

This is not an accusation. The Fed has a domestic mandate set by Congress. It is doing its job correctly by that mandate. The problem is that its job, as legally defined, does not include responsibility for the externalities its decisions create for the rest of the world. That gap is a design flaw in the international financial architecture, not a failure of any individual institution.

## **Market Notes Take**

The Fed's mandate is domestic. That is a legal and institutional fact. And within that mandate, its conduct of monetary policy through the 2022–2025 cycle has been broadly defensible — it was responding to the most significant domestic inflationary episode in four decades.

But the Fed's decisions are de facto global policy, whether or not that is the intent. The capital flows, the currency pressures, the imported inflation, the constrained monetary independence of small open economies — these are not abstract externalities. They are price increases on kitchen tables in Bogotá, Lima, and Buenos Aires paid for by communities that had no representative in the room where the decision was made.

The international community needs better coordination mechanisms for monetary policy spillovers. The current system was built for another era. Reforming it will require the United States to accept constraints on unilateral monetary policy action that it has never historically accepted — which means reform will come slowly, if at all. In the meantime, the communities paying the price are the ones with the least voice in the room. That is not a sustainable arrangement.

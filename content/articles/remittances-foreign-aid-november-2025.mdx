The World Bank's November 2025 remittance data confirmed a pattern that has now held for five consecutive years: the money sent home by migrants to their families in developing countries — $850 billion globally in 2025 — dwarfs official development assistance by a ratio of approximately 3.5 to 1. ODA from OECD donor countries totaled around $240 billion in the same period. The gap is not closing.

This is not a new finding. The remittance-ODA differential has been documented consistently since the early 2000s, and the story is familiar to anyone in development finance or international economics. But the consistency of the pattern, and the consistency with which it fails to produce meaningful policy change, is worth examining directly.

## What Remittances Actually Are

Remittances are not charity. They are not foreign investment. They are wages earned by workers in wealthier countries, transferred to family members in lower-income countries who depend on them for housing, food, education, and healthcare. In several of the world's largest remittance-receiving economies — the Philippines, El Salvador, Guatemala, Nepal, Tajikistan — remittances represent between 15% and 35% of GDP. They are, in measurable terms, the most significant source of foreign exchange and household income support in these countries.

Unlike foreign aid, remittances are direct: the money goes from a worker's account to their mother's account, not through a government ministry, an implementing partner, a regional subcontractor, and a local procurement process. Unlike foreign investment, remittances are counter-cyclical: when the economy in the recipient country worsens, remittances tend to increase as migrants send more to support struggling families. Unlike official development lending, remittances do not create sovereign debt.

The efficiency of remittances as a development tool is not a contested claim. The empirical evidence is overwhelming. The returns to households in terms of education spending, healthcare utilization, and small business formation are well-documented across multiple contexts.

## The Fee-Gouge Problem Is Entirely Policy-Solvable

And yet: the average global cost of sending a remittance was approximately 6.2% in 2025, according to World Bank data. For certain corridors — particularly South-to-South routes and transfers to smaller receiving economies — the cost exceeds 10%. The Sustainable Development Goals set a target of 3% by 2030. The world is not on track to hit it.

This is not a technical problem. Digital payment infrastructure capable of executing sub-1% cross-border transfers exists and has existed for several years. What maintains costs at 6% is a combination of: incumbent financial institutions protecting their correspondent banking margins, regulatory environments in both sending and receiving countries that create compliance costs, and a political environment in which migrant workers — a population without concentrated lobbying power in most sending-country legislatures — are not a constituency anyone has strong incentives to fight for.

The regulatory barriers are particularly frustrating because they are often justified in the name of anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing compliance. These are real concerns. But the proportionality of applying them to a Honduran construction worker sending $300 to his family in Tegucigalpa is questionable. The effect is to tax the most vulnerable participants in the international financial system for the structural convenience of financial institutions and regulatory agencies.

## Development Finance Is Missing Its Most Powerful Tool

The development finance community — multilateral banks, bilateral aid agencies, impact investors — invests enormous resources in projects designed to produce development outcomes that remittances produce organically, at scale, and without overhead. There is an irony in funding a study on household food security in Guatemala while simultaneously maintaining a regulatory environment that takes $18 out of every $300 transferred from a Guatemalan migrant in Atlanta to their family in Huehuetenango.

## **Market Notes Take**

Remittances are the most efficient development tool on the planet. The evidence is not ambiguous on this. And they are systematically taxed, fee-gouged, over-regulated, and under-studied by the same international community that spends billions on development programming that cannot match their reach or their efficiency.

If policymakers were serious about community impact — and by "serious" I mean willing to take on the incumbent financial institutions and regulatory bodies that benefit from the status quo — they would start here. Cut the cost of sending money home. Harmonize correspondent banking regulations to reduce the compliance burden on small-value transfers. Create regulatory sandboxes for digital remittance providers in high-cost corridors. Build measurement frameworks that treat remittances as a strategic development resource rather than a footnote to the ODA figures.

The $850 billion is already there. It's already moving. It's already changing lives. Everything the development community struggles to accomplish through projects, programs, and partnerships, remittances do quietly, daily, at the individual level, with near-zero overhead. The only obstacle to doing it better is the willingness to fight for the people doing the sending. Everything else is theater.

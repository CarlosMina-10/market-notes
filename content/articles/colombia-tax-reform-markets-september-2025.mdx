Colombian bond markets moved sharply in September 2025 as President Gustavo Petro's government unveiled the latest iteration of its fiscal reform proposal — a package that includes higher taxes on financial transactions, expanded wealth levies, and new royalty arrangements on extractive industries. The sovereign's 10-year dollar bond spread over U.S. Treasuries widened by approximately 45 basis points in the week following the announcement, a meaningful move that reflects a market reassessing Colombia's political risk premium.

The reaction was not a surprise to anyone who has been watching the Petro administration's fiscal signaling over the past year. Markets had been pricing Colombia's credit on the assumption that the government's more ambitious reform agenda would face congressional resistance sufficient to moderate its most market-unfriendly components. The September announcement suggested the government was prepared to push harder than that consensus view anticipated.

## Colombia's Fundamentals Are Genuinely Solid

Before addressing the market reaction, it is worth stating clearly what Colombia's fiscal fundamentals actually look like, because the spread widening risk obscuring some important context. Colombia's public debt-to-GDP ratio, while elevated relative to pre-pandemic levels, remains below the LatAm regional average. The central bank — Banco de la República — has maintained its independence and executed a credible tightening cycle that has brought headline inflation from its 2023 peak back toward the target range. Colombia's export base, while commodity-concentrated, has diversified meaningfully over the past decade.

These are real strengths. A country with Colombia's fundamental position and institutional track record should not, in most analytical frameworks, be trading at the spread levels that September 2025 has produced. The spread widening is not a verdict on Colombia's ability to service its debt. It is a verdict on political risk and execution credibility — a different and, in some ways, more correctable problem.

## Why Execution Signals Matter as Much as Policy Content

The market's concern is not fundamentally about whether a financial transaction tax or expanded wealth levy is good or bad economic policy — reasonable economists disagree on that, and the empirical literature is genuinely mixed. The concern is about the process through which fiscal reform is being designed and communicated, and what that process signals about the stability of the policy environment.

Fiscal reform that is credible, technically designed with input from the finance ministry and central bank, negotiated with Congress through a transparent process, and communicated with clear implementation timelines does not typically produce 45 basis point spread widening. Fiscal reform that appears to be driven by presidential initiative without full institutional coordination, that changes scope and framing across multiple iterations, and that arrives without detailed implementation mechanisms does produce that reaction — because it signals process risk as much as policy risk.

Investors are not always right. But they are correctly reading the signal that the reform's design and communication process sends about how fiscal policy will be managed in Colombia for the next three years. The policy may be correct. The process has undermined the market's confidence in its predictability.

---

## **Market Notes Take**

Colombia has the fundamentals to weather this — the debt dynamics are manageable, the central bank is credible, and the underlying economy is not in distress. Investors who are selling Colombian bonds purely on political noise are likely leaving risk premium on the table that will compress when the reform cycle stabilizes.

But they are right to be cautious in the near term, and not because the reform is inherently wrong. Tax reform in an unequal middle-income country that finances too much of its fiscal position from extractive industries is structurally necessary. Petro is not inventing that problem.

The issue is execution signals. Markets are pricing the risk that policy ambiguity and institutional friction in the reform process will generate fiscal outcomes worse than the counterfactual — not because the goals are bad, but because the process isn't building the confidence needed for durable implementation. Until the execution signals improve, the spread premium is rational, even if the policy ambition is justified.

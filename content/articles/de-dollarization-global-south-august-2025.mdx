The BRICS summit discussions in the summer of 2025 returned to a theme that has been building momentum for years: the fragility of dependence on the U.S. dollar as the world's singular reserve and trade settlement currency. The conversations ranged from technical mechanisms for bilateral trade settlement in local currencies, to longer-term proposals for a shared settlement unit, to expanded use of the New Development Bank as a non-dollar financing channel for member states.

The Western financial press coverage of these discussions has been predictably dismissive. "Dollar dominance is secure," the headlines read, often supported by data on dollar share of global reserve holdings, which remains north of 58%. The subtext is clear: de-dollarization is either a geopolitical fantasy or an anti-American project driven by revisionist powers. Neither framing is accurate, and both are analytically lazy.

## Why De-dollarization Is Rational Risk Management

To understand why countries in the Global South are increasingly interested in reducing their dollar exposure, it helps to understand what dollar dependence costs in practice. It costs when the Fed tightens U.S. monetary policy and capital flows out of emerging markets, weakening local currencies, raising import costs, and triggering inflation in economies that had no role in creating the inflationary pressure being addressed. It costs when dollar-denominated sovereign debt, accumulated during periods of cheap dollar liquidity, becomes punishingly expensive to service when U.S. rates rise. It costs when access to the dollar payment system is weaponized as a geopolitical tool — as it has been against Iran, Russia, and Venezuela — creating existential uncertainty about financial access for any country that finds itself on the wrong side of U.S. foreign policy.

These are not hypothetical costs. They are historical facts with documented, quantified impacts on sovereign fiscal positions, household purchasing power, and economic growth rates. Countries with the financial sophistication to diversify away from these risks have rational incentives to do so, independent of any political sentiment toward the United States.

## The Dollar Isn't Dying — But Its Grip Is Loosening

The analytically honest position is this: the dollar is not being replaced. There is no plausible candidate currency that combines the dollar's market depth, institutional infrastructure, and track record as a store of value. The renminbi lacks financial account convertibility. The euro is structurally constrained by political fragmentation. Gold is not a functioning settlement mechanism at scale.

What is changing is the dollar's unchallenged monopoly on the marginal trade and reserve decision. Countries are building optionality — bilateral swap lines, local currency trade agreements, alternative payment systems — not to replace the dollar entirely, but to reduce the cost of dollar dependence during periods of U.S. monetary tightening or geopolitical volatility.

That is a meaningful structural shift even if it doesn't produce a "dollar collapse" narrative. The dollar's share of global reserves fell from 71% in 1999 to 58% in 2025 — a 13-percentage-point decline over a quarter century that most dollar bulls wave away as noise. It is not noise. It is a slow, structural, directional shift that compounds over decades.

## The Community Impact Is Real and Underreported

The human cost of dollar dependence is rarely quantified in the macro discourse. In dollarized economies, when the U.S. raises rates, local wages and purchasing power erode without any domestic policy lever available to offset it. In economies with dollar-pegged exchange rates, the cost of the peg in terms of foregone monetary policy flexibility falls on workers and households, not on the institutional investors who hold the dollar assets that make the peg attractive.

The communities bearing the cost of dollar dependence are rarely the ones designing the financial systems that create it. De-dollarization, at its core, is an effort to redistribute some of that cost and risk back to a more equitable position.

---

## **Market Notes Take**

De-dollarization isn't anti-American — it's a rational risk management response from countries burned by dollar-denominated debt cycles, Fed spillover effects, and the geopolitical weaponization of financial access. The people framing it as ideological antagonism are either not paying attention or have institutional reasons to dismiss the trend.

The dollar isn't dying. But its grip is loosening — and that matters. The marginal de-dollarization happening right now in bilateral trade agreements, NDB lending, and reserve diversification decisions is not the endgame. It is the infrastructure being laid for a world where dollar dependence is a choice rather than a structural inevitability. That is a better world for most of the countries that have to live with the consequences of U.S. monetary policy without having a vote in setting it.
